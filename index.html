Drug Crime in San Francisco

Drug-related crime has been a central issue in California politics. But how much of what we see in the data reflects policy — and how much reflects deeper structural change?

1. Changes Over Time

We begin by examining how drug-related crimes have evolved over the past two decades.

Annual drug and narcotic crime incidents in San Francisco, 2003–2025.
Question

Why did drug crime drop so sharply around 2010 — and what drove the surge after 2021?

Note

There is a sharp drop between 2009–2011, followed by a rise after 2021 and another spike around 2025. These shifts align with major policy periods.

2. A Shift in Where Crime Happens

Total crime counts alone do not tell the full story. We now turn to geography.

Press Play to watch how drug crime shifts across San Francisco's neighborhoods over time
Geographic distribution of drug and narcotic crimes by neighborhood, 2003–2025.
Question

Is drug crime becoming increasingly concentrated in specific neighborhoods?

Finding

Over time, drug crime becomes increasingly concentrated in Tenderloin, Southern, and Mission — what was once distributed across the city is now localized.

3. Comparing with Other Crimes

Is this geographic concentration unique to drug crime, or part of a broader citywide trend?

Percentage of cases by district for selected crime categories, 2003–2025.
Question

Do other types of crime show the same pattern of geographic concentration?

Finding

Other crimes remain relatively stable in their geographic distribution — suggesting the concentration is specific to drug-related activity, not a citywide shift.

Conclusion

Drug crime in San Francisco has changed not just in scale, but in structure.

The increasing spatial concentration points to deeper dynamics — enforcement, displacement, or demographic change — that go well beyond overall crime levels.
